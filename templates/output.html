Overview

Vitamin A is an essential nutrient for vision, immune system function, and skin health. It can be found in two primary forms: preformed vitamin A (retinol) from animal products and provitamin A (carotenoids) from plant-based foods.

Symptoms o
  • Night blindness (difficulty seeing in low light)
  • Dry eyes (xerophthalmia)
  • Dry skin and hair
  • Bitot's spots (foamy patches on the whites of the eyes)
  • Frequent infections
  • Delayed growth in children
  • Throat and chest infections
  • Acne and breakouts
Show more
Health Risks o
  • Permanent blindness
  • Increased mortality, especially in children
  • Compromised immune system
  • Increased risk of respiratory and diarrheal infections
  • Anemia
  • Growth retardation
  • Reproductive issues
  • Increased risk of maternal mortality
Show more
Suggested Food o

Animal Sources (Preformed Vitamin A):

  • Liver (beef, chicken, fish)
  • Cod liver oil
  • Egg yolks
  • Dairy products (milk, cheese, butter)

Plant Sources (Beta-carotene):

  • Orange and yellow vegetables (carrots, sweet potatoes, pumpkin)
  • Dark leafy greens (spinach, kale, collard greens)
  • Orange fruits (mangoes, papaya, apricots)
  • Red bell peppers
Show more
Tips o
  • Include at least one vitamin A-rich food in your daily diet
  • Pair plant sources with a small amount of healthy fat to improve absorption
  • Cook carrots and other vegetables lightly to preserve nutrients
  • Consider a multivitamin if your diet is limited
  • Consult with a healthcare provider before starting supplements
  • Store vitamin A-rich vegetables in a cool, dark place
  • Pregnant women should ensure adequate intake but avoid excessive supplementation
  • Regular eye check-ups can help detect early signs of deficiency
Show more

Prevention of Vitamin A Deficiency

Vitamin A deficiency can be prevented by maintaining a balanced diet with adequate sources of the vitamin. It is especially important for individuals who have limited access to fresh fruits and vegetables, pregnant women, and young children.

Treatment for Vitamin A Deficiency

If diagnosed with a Vitamin A deficiency, a healthcare provider may recommend vitamin A supplements or dietary changes to address the deficiency. In severe cases, higher doses of vitamin A may be administered under medical supervision.

Vitamin B Deficiency

Overview

Vitamin B refers to a group of eight B vitamins that play vital roles in energy production, brain function, and cell metabolism. These include B1 (thiamine), B2 (riboflavin), B3 (niacin), B5, B6, B7, B9 (folate), and B12. Deficiency in one or more can lead to serious health issues.

Symptoms o
  • Fatigue and weakness
  • Irritability and mood changes
  • Tingling or numbness in hands/feet
  • Confusion or memory problems
  • Sores around the mouth
  • Poor coordination
Show more
Health Risks o
  • Anemia
  • Nerve damage (neuropathy)
  • Cognitive decline
  • Heart disease
  • Birth defects (especially folate deficiency)
  • Impaired metabolism
Show more
Suggested Food o

Animal Sources:

  • Eggs
  • Dairy products
  • Fish and poultry
  • Red meat

Plant Sources:

  • Whole grains
  • Leafy green vegetables
  • Legumes (beans, lentils)
  • Nuts and seeds
  • Fortified cereals
Show more
Tips o
  • Include a mix of animal and plant-based foods
  • Look for fortified cereals if vegetarian
  • Don't overcook vegetables — preserve B vitamins
  • Consider a B-complex supplement if advised
  • Pregnant women should monitor folate intake
  • Avoid excessive alcohol which depletes B vitamins
Show more

Prevention of Vitamin B Deficiency

Consume a varied diet rich in whole grains, leafy vegetables, and animal products. Fortified foods and B-complex supplements may be necessary for vegetarians or people with absorption issues.

Treatment for Vitamin B Deficiency

Treatment depends on the specific B vitamin deficiency. It may include oral supplements, dietary adjustments, or injections (especially for B12). Seek medical evaluation for proper diagnosis.

Vitamin B12 Deficiency

Overview

Vitamin B12 (cobalamin) is crucial for nerve tissue health, brain function, and the production of red blood cells. Since it is mostly found in animal products, vegetarians and vegans are at a higher risk of deficiency.

Symptoms o
  • Fatigue and weakness
  • Tingling or numbness in hands and feet
  • Difficulty walking or balance issues
  • Memory loss and cognitive difficulties
  • Pale or jaundiced skin
  • Glossitis and mouth ulcers
Show more
Health Risks o
  • Pernicious anemia
  • Neurological damage
  • Impaired brain function
  • Depression and mood disorders
  • Increased risk of heart disease (via homocysteine)
Show more
Suggested Food o
  • Eggs and dairy (milk, cheese, yogurt)
  • Fish (salmon, trout, tuna)
  • Meat and poultry (chicken, beef, liver)
  • Fortified cereals and plant milks
  • Nutritional yeast (fortified)
Show more
Tips o
  • Include animal-based products regularly (or fortified options)
  • Vegans should consider B12 supplements
  • Check B12 levels if experiencing fatigue or tingling
  • Pair B12-rich foods with a balanced diet for absorption
  • Consult a doctor before starting B12 injections or high-dose supplements
Show more

Prevention of Vitamin B12 Deficiency

Eat a diet rich in animal products or fortified plant-based options. Vegans and older adults should consider regular B12 supplements or injections as advised by a doctor.

Treatment for Vitamin B12 Deficiency

Treatment includes oral supplements or intramuscular B12 injections in severe cases. Long-term management depends on diet, absorption ability, and medical conditions like pernicious anemia.

Vitamin C Deficiency

Overview

Vitamin C (ascorbic acid) is essential for collagen synthesis, immune function, and iron absorption. It also acts as an antioxidant, protecting cells from damage. Deficiency can result in weakened immunity and connective tissue problems.

Symptoms o
  • Fatigue and weakness
  • Dry and splitting hair
  • Swollen and bleeding gums
  • Slow wound healing
  • Easy bruising
  • Frequent infections
Show more
Health Risks o
  • Scurvy
  • Impaired immune function
  • Joint pain and swelling
  • Iron deficiency anemia (due to poor absorption)
  • Skin issues
Show more
Suggested Food o
  • Citrus fruits (oranges, lemons)
  • Strawberries
  • Kiwi
  • Bell peppers
  • Broccoli
  • Tomatoes
  • Leafy greens
Show more
Tips o
  • Eat fresh fruits and veggies daily
  • Drink citrus-based juices in the morning
  • Avoid overcooking – vitamin C is heat-sensitive
  • Add lemon to your salads or water
  • Store fruits in cool, dry areas to retain nutrients
Show more

Prevention of Vitamin C Deficiency

Consume a variety of fruits and vegetables, especially citrus fruits and colorful produce. Incorporate raw salads and juices to maintain vitamin C levels.

Treatment for Vitamin C Deficiency

Treatment includes dietary improvements and vitamin C supplementation. Severe deficiencies like scurvy may require high-dose supplementation under medical supervision.

Vitamin D Deficiency

Overview

Vitamin D helps the body absorb calcium and is critical for bone health, immune function, and mood regulation. It's synthesized by the skin when exposed to sunlight and is also available in certain foods.

Symptoms o
  • Bone pain and muscle weakness
  • Fatigue and tiredness
  • Mood changes or depression
  • Frequent illnesses or infections
  • Hair loss
  • Back pain
Show more
Health Risks o
  • Osteoporosis
  • Rickets in children
  • Fractures and falls in older adults
  • Weakened immune response
  • Cardiovascular diseases
Show more
Suggested Food o
  • Fatty fish (salmon, tuna, mackerel)
  • Egg yolks
  • Cheese
  • Fortified milk and plant-based alternatives
  • Fortified cereals and juices
  • Mushrooms exposed to sunlight
Show more
Tips o
  • Spend 15–20 minutes in the sun daily
  • Include vitamin D-rich foods in meals
  • Consider supplements during winter
  • Check vitamin D levels if you're often tired
  • Combine with calcium for stronger bones
Show more

Prevention of Vitamin D Deficiency

Ensure moderate sun exposure, consume fortified foods, and maintain a balanced diet. Consider supplementation in low sunlight regions or for individuals with limited sun exposure.

Treatment for Vitamin D Deficiency

Oral vitamin D supplements, dietary adjustments, and increased safe sun exposure are recommended. Severe deficiency may require high-dose therapy prescribed by a healthcare professional.

  • Impaired immune system
Show more

  • Sunflower seeds
  • Spinach
  • Avocados
  • Peanuts
  • Wheat germ oil
  • Olive oil
Show more
Tips o
  • Include nuts and seeds in snacks
  • Drizzle salads with healthy oils
  • Combine vitamin E with vitamin C for synergy
  • Choose unprocessed, fresh produce
  • Limit processed foods which deplete antioxidants
Show more

Prevention of Vitamin E Deficiency

Consume a balanced diet rich in healthy fats, nuts, seeds, and green leafy vegetables. Cooking with natural oils like olive oil also helps maintain levels.

Treatment for Vitamin E Deficiency

Supplementation is the primary treatment, especially for those with absorption disorders. Dietary changes should also support long-term levels and health benefits.
